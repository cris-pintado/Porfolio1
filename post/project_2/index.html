Project 2: Experimental Approaches for Geometric Correction

January, 2024

Goal of the project:

Experimental outlook for geometric correction of various remote imaging sources utilizing manual and automatics approach.

Introduction:

This project tries to convey the procedure of geometric corrections of satellite images that are not georeferenced directly, and are assigned arbitrary coordinate units of pixels, and converting them into a desired georeferenced coordinate system, using both manual and automatic models.

Resources Used:

  • Landsat-5, Thematic Mapper, 7 channels, nominal resolution 120 m to 30 m. Date of recruitment: 05-15-1992. Center of scene (UTM in m): X: 350133.218750 Y: 4624794.500000.
  • Orthophotos of the PNOA a 15 m and 30 m spatial resolution
  • Digital Elevation Model (30 m), created from mosaic of the MDE of 5 m spatial resolution of the IGN
  • Photographic images to be corrected (3 bands for RGB 24 bits, 1 band for gray scale or para color con palette). Date: May 23, 1993. Format IMG, scanned at 24 bits and taken with a focal length of 153.87 mm.
  • Digital image mapping: 4 orthophotos 1:5000 (ICC).
  • Digital Model of Elevations (45 m grid) that cover the scope of the photo to georeference (ICC). Approximate year: 1991.

    • Software: MiraMon GIS&RS v9.1a

    Methodology process of correction

    Manual correction was done to the Landsat-5 images, Thematic Mapper, 7 bands, nominal resolution of 120m to 30m taken on 15-05-1992. The correction of image was conducted through the placement of check points, control points, and tie point, sourced from a various cartographic references including an orthophoto from the PNOA with resolution of 15m/30m, a topographic map from the ICGC at a scale of 1:25 000, and GPX track points.

    Unlike correction of polynomial raster where crossing of reference networks are used to position GCP, for a digitalized satellite imaged that is not the case and required a visual estimation in comparison to different georeferenced images.

    Manual Geometric Correction of Landsat-7

    I began my correction by analyzing the orthophoto PNOA and comparing it to the Landsat images, over a variety of different bands combination such as: (IRp,IRm1,R), (RGB), and (IRm2,G,B) which helped identify accurately ground characteristics such as similar vegetation formations, a clear distinction over water areas and more defined coast line. With this I began adding tie points for both the control points and check points. What I found to be more accurate was to look for water reservoirs, over river intersections and urban buildings with sharp angles.

    My first problem encountered was comprehending the different obtainment dates of both images, since this particular Landsat Images are from 1992 and the PNOA are from the 2017, resulting in different ground characteristic like change in river currents, and creating of new urban like seaports.

    I also obtained 2 tie points using a GPX and documenting them in the area near La Sagrada Familia and near Parc de Ciutadella. For tie points using topographic map from the ICGC at a scale of 1:25 000 I focused on the area of the Olympic Cable Park and Applus IDIADA speed circuit. Is important to add that for GPX coordinates were originally displayed in a reference system of Longitude-latitude with WGS84 datum which required me to change the cartographic projection to align with the desired reference system of UTM zone 31 Northern Hemisphere with ETRS89 datum.

    After having both my control point and check point on the uncorrected system I began by converting those VEC points into a COR (CorrGeom) for both files and assigning them DEM of 30m with a resampling method of nearest neighbor. After having both files, I transferred the data of the test file to the GCP file to fit with parameters of the CorrGeom application of generating a spatial orthoimage.

    To have all bands of the multiband raster simultaneously geometrically corrected I used the CorrGEom application previously stated, inputting my now combined COR file, and adding a MDE raster file which will define the size of the pixel of 30m and the type of correction in rows was set to a 1rst degree without Z.

    Manual Geometric Correction of Landsat-7

    To have a better understanding of these processes I also decided to geometrically correct the Landsat – 7 Enhanced Thematic Mapper Plus images of 8 bands, taken on 30-06-2002. This process was similar to the previous with the difference being of an area of low visibility and contains 8 bands with the 8th band being a panchromatic band with the highest resolution. The correction of images was conducted though the placement of check points, control points, and tie point, sourced only from the orthophoto from the PNOA with resolution of 15m/30m.

    First by trying to identify the location of the image, which I did by sectionizing a part of the PNOA with a width of 20 Km and going frame by frame from top to bottom until I could find similar characteristic to the uncorrected image.

    Once I was able to locate the image: (UTM-31N ETRS89)

In this case I benefited from the panchromatic band which had the best resolution of them all. Then converting those VEC points into a COR (CorrGeom) for both control points and test points.

    Finally I used the CorrGEom application previously stated, inputting my now combined COR file, and adding a MDE raster file which will define the size of the pixel of 30m and the type of correction in rows was set to a 1rst degree without Z.

    Correction by automatically placing homologous points with MiraMon's CorGeo2 app

    For the automatic portion of the exercise require to use the application CorGeo2 which was downloaded and placed in the MiraMon directory. Once I understood that the application function over a set of commands, I developed a syntax that details, the what, and the how I wanted my image to be corrected.

    Batch script using parameters from CorGeo2 in Miramon

    
    cmd -> C:\MiraMon\CorrGeo2.exe D:\corrgeo\results D:\corrgeo\19831_19920515_1.img 4 0
    D:\corrgeo\198031_OrtoPNOA_ETRS89_30m.img
    D:\corrgeo\MDT_ASTER_IGN_198_31_UTM31ETRS89_30m_v2.img
    D:\corrgeo\MDT_ASTER_IGN_198_31_UTM31ETRS89_300m_v2.img 350133.218750 4624794.500000
    UTM-31N-ETRS89 /INTERVAL_Z=50 /N_MOSTRES_XY=35 /SAC /MUT

    The command required me to adapt my MDE raster to have a pixel density of 300m because is the model from which the point bank will be generated with a lower resolution for an image of 30 m per pixel. I specified the integers “m” and “n” to reflect the altitude intervals and number of samples to choose an interval of 50 and sample of 35 to be able to get the best result correction over a densify portion of points.

    The system calculated 478 control points and 452 check points. As expected both control points and check points present a MCF lower than 0.5 and a total RMS of 12.99 m which is more than appropriate error as it only presents an error of less the half a pixel.

    In conclusion, this exercise undertakes the aimed to demonstrate the intricate process of geometric corrections of satellite images lacking georeferencing. The manual geometric corrections involved a detailed analysis of ground features, the adjustment and refinement to achieve a Root Mean Square (RMS) error within acceptable limits. The automatic correction, utilizing the CorGeo2 application, exhibited a more favorable RMS error below half a pixels, and seen by the dispersion of set points on hard to see part of the images indicates the favorableness of automated approach when it comes to a manual selection of point as seen when compared to manual correction of Landsat -7, facilitating both control point selection and continuous refinement in achieving accurate geometric correction for satellite images.
