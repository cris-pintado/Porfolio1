A study on tomato plants-Botrytis Cinerea interactions.

December 1, 2013

Description 🔬🧬🧫🌱🌾:

The goal of this project was to better understand the role of a set of tomato pathogenesis-related genes (PR genes) agaist an economically devastating fungus (B.cinerea).

Two main aspects of these genes were studied:

1. PR gene expression level by q-RT-PCR:

During this experiment, we analized the constitutive expression level of the PR genes in different tomato plant parts (Figure 1). In order to do so, plant samples were immediately snap-frozen and crushed in liquid nitrogen and stored at -80°C. This was followed by RNA isolation and subsequent cDNA synthesis. Level of mRNA coding for these proteins was assessed by quantitative Reverse Transcription-Polymerase Chain Reaction (q-RT-PCR).

Figure 1: Comparison of the expression level of all putative homologue PR genes under study in different plant parts-organs. mRNA expression was determined by q-RT-PCR. Error bars represent SEM from the mean. Data are expressed as normalized ΔΔCT values. Results are mean values from three individual experiments (n=3).

2. Functional characterization of the PR genes using VIGS:

The phenotypic analysis of VIGS plants impaired in the expression of PR genes. Below, VIGS technique is explained in detail. Virus-induced gene silencing (VIGS) is a molecular technique, widely used for gene function studies in plants. This technique exploits post-transcriptional gene silencing, a well conserved defence mechanism that plants use for protection against viruses and transposons (See fig below).

Figure 2: Schematic representation of the post transcriptional gene silencing (PTGS) process in a plant cell. Viral double-stranded RNA is processed by the cellular factor Dicer to form siRNAs. These RNAs are used by a RNAi silencing complex (RISC) to specifically recognize and degrade the viral single-stranded RNA

VIGS technique requires using a virus as vector. The trobavirus Tobacco Rattle Virus (TRV) displays broad host range and it is the most widely used in gene silencing in Solanaceae species such as tomato, potato or tobacco. The ability of TRV to rapidly reproduce and spread throughout the entire plant while inducing an efficient gene silencing makes this virus a suitable VIGS vector. The key to this effectiveness appears to be a balanced host silencing system, which allows TRV to reproduce, as well as permits the post transcriptional gene silencing process to take place. In addition, plants infected with TRV react mildly to the virus. The TRV genome is composed by two linear RNA fragments. RNA1 encodes the replicase and movement proteins, while RNA2 encodes the coat proteins and two more non-structural proteins. In the VIGS technique, RNA1 and RNA2 sequences are modified and cloned between the T-DNA of Agrobacterium tumefaciens. pTRV2 contains the target gene sequence. In order to get an abundant initial inoculum of viral particles and ensure the propagation of the virus along the plant cells, young plants are infiltrated with A. tumefaciens transformed with pTRV1 and pTRV2 in a 1:1 ratio (Figure 3).

Figure 3: VIGS technique using tobacco rattle virus (TRV). pTRV1 and pTRV2 are T-DNA plasmids that encode RNA1 and RNA2 respectively. pTRV1 contains RdRP (RNA dependent RNA polymerase), M (Movement protein) and M3 (A Non-structural viral cysteine-rich protein). pTRV2 contains CP (coat protein) and the target sequence we want to silence. These genes are under the control of cauliflower mosaic virus 35S (a strong promoter). These plasmids are propagated in Agrobacterium tumefaciens. When plants are infected with these transformed Agrobacterium, the DNA between LB and RB borders is inserted into the plant genome. This leads to a massive production of TRV. The posttranscriptional gene silencing plant machinery will knock out the target sequence which we previously inserted into RNA2 genome. Positive control ΔPDS plants showed a visible phenotype of gene silencing typically 2 weeks after vacuum infiltration.

Recombinant viruses can be used to knockdown a specific plant gene by inserting a host gene target sequence within their genome. VIGS efficiency varies greatly depending on the species and plant tissues. Therefore, reporter genes are needed. Among them, phytoene desaturase (PDS) has been the most commonly used. This is an important enzyme for carotenoids synthesis. Consequently, PDS silencing leads to a white, photobleached phenotype that can be easily followed visually. See Figure 4 below:
